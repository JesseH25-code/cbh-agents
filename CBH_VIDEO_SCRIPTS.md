## SCRIPT 1 — Deal Structure (the niche's #1 topic)

**Title options**
1. How Deals Actually Get Structured When You Sell Your Business (Cash, Seller Notes & Earnouts)
2. "All Cash" Isn't the Best Offer — Here's How Smart Sellers Get Paid
3. The 3 Ways You Get Paid When You Sell Your Business (And Which One Wins)

**Thumbnail text:** `CASH vs SELLER NOTE vs EARNOUT` / `WHICH PAYS MORE?`
**Length:** 8–10 min · **Primary CTA:** free BOV

> Framing rule: Jesse explains structures as the *advisor*. CBH is not a lender and does not "offer seller financing." Language is always "here's how buyers structure this" / "here's what I'd negotiate for you."

**HOOK (0:00–0:25)**
"Most owners think the best offer is the biggest number. It's not. I've seen a $4 million all-cash offer leave the seller with *less* than a $4.5 million deal that was structured right — after tax, after risk, after the dust settled. Today I'm going to walk you through exactly how a business sale actually gets paid out, the three main structures, and how to tell which one actually puts the most money in your pocket. I'm Jesse Hastings with CBH Business Group — we sell service businesses across Florida and we talk to a network of over 4,000 buyers every single day."

**BEAT 1 — Why structure matters more than the headline number (0:25–1:30)**
- The "price" is a story; the *structure* is the reality.
- Two levers every deal balances: how much cash at close vs. how much risk the seller carries.
- Tax treatment changes your real take-home — a structured deal can beat a bigger all-cash number.

**BEAT 2 — The three ways you get paid (1:30–5:30)**
1. **Cash at close** — cleanest, lowest risk to you, but buyers discount for it and lenders cap how much they'll fund. Most "all-cash" deals are really *bank-financed* deals.
2. **Seller note (seller financing)** — you carry a portion at interest. *Explain as advisor:* buyers love it because it signals confidence; sellers use it to bridge a price gap and often net more *with* interest. The keys I negotiate: short term, fair rate, personal guarantee, security. "This is a lever — not something we lend; it's something I structure into the deal for you."
3. **Earnout** — part of the price tied to future performance. Powerful for bridging a valuation gap, dangerous if the targets are vague. I make the metrics clean and inside your control.

**BEAT 3 — How to tell which one wins (5:30–7:30)**
- It comes down to: your timeline, your risk tolerance, the quality/verifiability of your numbers, and how many buyers are competing.
- Real example (CBH story, de-identified): a plumbing company looked weak on paper because of owner add-backs; we recast the financials, structured a modest seller note to bridge the gap, and closed at 4.1x normalized EBITDA — the owner walked with ~$800K more than he expected going in.
- The punchline: **competition + structure beats a single big number.** One buyer gives you their number; four buyers competing gives you yours.

## SCRIPT 2 — Valuation (the evergreen search winner)

**Title options**
1. What Is Your Business Actually Worth? (How Buyers Really Set the Price)
2. How to Value a Service Business in 2026 (Free Calculator Inside)
3. The Real Math Behind What Your Business Will Sell For

**Thumbnail text:** `WHAT'S IT WORTH?` / `EBITDA × ? = PRICE`
**Length:** 6–8 min · **Primary CTA:** valuation calculator → BOV

**HOOK (0:00–0:25)**
"There are two numbers in your head right now: what you *think* your business is worth, and what a buyer will *actually* pay. The gap between them is usually six or seven figures — and it's almost always fixable. Let me show you exactly how buyers value a business so you stop guessing. I'm Jesse Hastings, CBH Business Group."

**BEAT 1 — The basic formula (0:25–2:00)**
- Value = **earnings × a multiple.** Two questions decide everything: *which* earnings number, and *what* multiple.
- Earnings: SDE for smaller owner-operated businesses, EBITDA as you scale. Keep it simple on screen — define each in one line.
- The multiple isn't random; it's a risk score.

**BEAT 2 — What moves the multiple (2:00–4:30)**
The 5 things buyers pay premiums for (data-listicle energy):
1. **Recurring / contracted revenue** — predictability.
2. **Low customer concentration** — no single client >~20%.
3. **The business runs without you** — owner-dependency is the #1 valuation killer.
4. **Clean, verifiable financials** — buyers buy confidence in your numbers.
5. **A real team / documented processes.**
Each one is a lever; missing ones are *discounts* you can fix before going to market.

**BEAT 3 — Where owners get it wrong (4:30–6:00)**
- Valuing on revenue instead of margin: $5M at 20% beats $8M at 8%.
- Add-backs done wrong: banks need proof, not tricks — over-aggressive add-backs blow up in due diligence.
- Comparing to a buddy's sale in a different industry/market.

**CTA (6:00–7:00)**
"Want a ballpark in 2 minutes? Use the free calculator at cbhbusinessgroup.com/valuation-calculator. Want the real number? We'll run a free Broker's Opinion of Value — no cost, no commitment. Book below."

**Shorts cutdowns:** (a) "Revenue vs margin — which sells for more"; (b) "The #1 thing that kills your valuation"; (c) "The 5 things buyers pay a premium for."

## SCRIPT 3 — Deal Killers (highest-engagement format: the data/loss listicle)

**Title options**
1. 5 Things That Kill a Business Sale (and Cost Owners Hundreds of Thousands)
2. Why Most Business Sales Fall Apart — The 5 Deal Killers

**Thumbnail text:** `5 DEAL KILLERS` / `#3 COSTS YOU $400K`
**Length:** 7–9 min · **Primary CTA:** free BOV

**HOOK (0:00–0:25)**
"Most business sales don't fall apart over price. They fall apart over five specific things — and I watch owners walk into every one of them. Any one of these can cost you hundreds of thousands of dollars or kill the deal entirely. Here are the five, and how to fix each before you ever go to market. Jesse Hastings, CBH Business Group."

**THE 5 (0:25–6:30)** — each: name it → why buyers run → quick fix → (where useful) a CBH example
1. **Customer concentration.** One client = 35% of revenue → a buyer discounted by $400K on the spot. We diversified over ~8 months and relaunched $600K higher.
2. **Owner dependency.** If everything runs through you, you're not selling a business — you're selling a job. Build a layer of management before you sell.
3. **Messy / unverifiable financials.** Buyers buy confidence in your numbers; recast EBITDA properly and get a clean data room ready *before* diligence, not during.
4. **A short or shaky lease.** Sounds small — it can deflate the whole valuation if a buyer can't count on the location. Lock in your lease/options first.
5. **Going to market with one buyer.** No competition = no leverage. A real process with multiple qualified buyers is what creates the bidding tension that gets you top dollar.
